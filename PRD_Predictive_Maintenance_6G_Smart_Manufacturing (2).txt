===============================================================================
1. EXECUTIVE PRODUCT SUMMARY
===============================================================================

FactoryGuard 6G is a predictive-maintenance and anomaly-intelligence platform for smart manufacturing environments. It analyzes machine sensor readings, production behavior, quality indicators, and 6G-network reliability signals to identify subtle abnormal patterns before they develop into serious operational problems.

The product is not limited to reporting current manufacturing efficiency. Its primary purpose is to answer five operational questions:

1. Which machines are behaving abnormally right now?
2. Is the abnormal behavior isolated, persistent, or escalating?
3. Which sensor or network signals are contributing to the risk?
4. Which machines should maintenance teams inspect first?
5. Is machine behavior improving after maintenance or intervention?

The platform must combine exploratory data analysis, machine-specific baseline modeling, unsupervised anomaly detection, temporal escalation analysis, explainable risk scoring, and an interactive Streamlit application.

The final product must be understandable to both technical and non-technical users. Every important visualization must explain:
- What happened
- Why it matters
- What signals contributed
- What action is recommended
- Whether the result is observed, model-derived, or a proxy estimate

The project must never present an anomaly as a confirmed mechanical failure unless a verified failure label exists. The supplied dataset has no explicit breakdown, downtime, repair, or component-failure event field. Therefore, this project will produce risk intelligence and early-warning indicators, not unsupported claims of confirmed failure prediction.

===============================================================================
2. BACKGROUND AND BUSINESS CONTEXT
===============================================================================

Modern intelligent factories depend on interconnected machines, industrial sensors, automated production systems, quality-control systems, and low-latency communications. In these environments:

- Machine failures may be rare but extremely costly.
- A small degradation can affect production speed, product quality, energy consumption, and safety.
- Traditional static thresholds cannot adapt to differences between machines, operating modes, or production conditions.
- Subtle multidimensional changes may appear long before a visible efficiency decline.
- Network latency and packet loss may affect the reliability and timeliness of machine communication.
- Maintenance teams need prioritized and explainable alerts rather than large volumes of raw sensor data.

A 6G-integrated factory can potentially support near-real-time sensing and decision-making. In this project, the 6G context is represented through network latency and packet-loss indicators. These fields will be analyzed as operational communication signals; the system must not claim to model a complete commercial 6G network stack.

The desired operational transition is:

Reactive maintenance
→ Scheduled maintenance
→ Condition-based maintenance
→ Predictive and risk-prioritized maintenance

===============================================================================
3. PROBLEM STATEMENT
===============================================================================

Manufacturing organizations face unexpected machine breakdowns, production interruption, high maintenance expenditure, quality defects, energy instability, and difficulty detecting early warning signs in noisy sensor data.

Traditional rule-based monitoring is inadequate because:

- Normal ranges vary across Machine_ID.
- Normal ranges vary across Operation_Mode.
- A value may be normal in isolation but abnormal in combination with other values.
- Early warning patterns may be gradual rather than abrupt.
- A single global threshold can produce excessive false alerts.
- A momentary spike may be harmless, while a persistent moderate deviation may be dangerous.
- Communication instability can coincide with operational instability.
- Current efficiency labels describe the present state but do not necessarily identify emerging health risk.

FactoryGuard 6G must learn machine- and mode-specific normal behavior, detect rare and abnormal combinations, monitor risk over time, and convert model outputs into prioritized maintenance decisions.

===============================================================================
4. PRODUCT VISION
===============================================================================

Create an operational-intelligence platform that enables factory supervisors, maintenance engineers, reliability teams, analysts, and government or executive stakeholders to identify emerging machine-health risks early, understand the reasons behind those risks, and prioritize inspection or intervention using evidence-based recommendations.

The platform should feel like a practical industrial decision-support system, not only a collection of charts or a classroom notebook.

===============================================================================
5. PRODUCT OBJECTIVES
===============================================================================

5.1 Primary Objectives

- Establish a reliable machine-specific and operation-mode-specific behavioral baseline.
- Detect rare, multidimensional, and temporally persistent anomalies.
- Generate a normalized anomaly score for every observation.
- Translate anomaly scores into Low, Medium, and High maintenance-risk levels.
- Detect machines whose risk is escalating over time.
- Identify the sensor, production, quality, or network signals contributing to each alert.
- Provide a prioritized high-risk machine list.
- Compare pre-maintenance and post-maintenance behavior where maintenance-mode observations permit a valid comparison.
- Communicate results clearly to non-technical users.
- Produce reproducible analytics and deployment-ready project outputs.

5.2 Secondary Objectives

- Analyze the relationship between network reliability and operational anomalies.
- Evaluate whether anomaly risk precedes future efficiency degradation.
- Provide configurable alert thresholds and time windows.
- Support exportable maintenance-alert and executive-summary reports.
- Prepare the architecture for future live-stream integration.

5.3 Non-Goals

The initial version must not:

- Claim confirmed equipment failure without failure-event ground truth.
- Claim exact remaining useful life without labeled degradation-to-failure sequences.
- Claim exact downtime or monetary savings without validated downtime and cost records.
- Diagnose a specific mechanical component when component-level sensor or repair data is unavailable.
- Control machinery automatically.
- Trigger autonomous shutdowns.
- Replace a qualified maintenance engineer.
- Present simulated streaming data as actual live factory data.
- Treat Efficiency_Status as the sole definition of machine health.

- The research paper, dashboard, executive summary, and model artifacts are consistent with one another.

Dataset:
Thales_Group_Manufacturing.csv

Observed dataset profile:
- Total records: 100,000
- Total columns: 14
- Unique machines: 50
- Date range: 1 January 2025 to 10 March 2025
- Missing values: 0
- Exact duplicate rows: 0
- Duplicate Machine_ID + timestamp combinations: 28
- Operation modes:
  - Active: 70,054 records
  - Idle: 20,057 records
  - Maintenance: 9,889 records
- Efficiency status:
  - Low: 77,825 records
  - Medium: 19,189 records
  - High: 2,986 records

Important dataset observations:
- The actual time column is named Timestamp, not Time.
- Operation_Mode values are Active, Idle, and Maintenance.
- Efficiency_Status is highly imbalanced toward Low.
- The dataset contains no explicit failure-event label.
- The dataset contains no downtime-duration field.
- The dataset contains no repair-action or maintenance-work-order identifier.
- The dataset contains no component type or failure mode.
- The dataset contains no direct monetary cost field.
- Machine observations are not evenly spaced per machine.
- Per-machine time gaps vary because records are distributed across machines.
- Some machines have more than one record at the same timestamp.
- Temporal features must therefore be calculated after sorting by Machine_ID and datetime.
- Time-based windows must use actual timestamps or carefully documented observation windows.

These constraints must be visible in the research paper and application documentation.

10. DATA QUALITY AND GOVERNANCE REQUIREMENTS
===============================================================================

10.1 Ingestion Validation

The system must:
- Verify that all expected columns exist.
- Validate numeric data types.
- Parse date and time safely.
- Reject or quarantine invalid timestamps.
- Detect missing values.
- Detect exact duplicate records.
- Detect duplicate Machine_ID + datetime records.
- Check impossible or out-of-range values.
- Confirm known categorical values.
- Produce a data-quality summary before modeling.

10.2 Duplicate Timestamp Handling

For duplicate Machine_ID + datetime observations:
- Do not silently delete records.
- Inspect whether measurements are identical.
- If identical, retain one and log the deduplication.
- If different, aggregate only with a documented method or assign a sequence identifier.
- Record the decision in the data-quality report.

10.3 Outlier Handling

Because the project is about anomaly detection:
- Do not automatically remove extreme values.
- Distinguish data errors from potentially meaningful anomalies.
- Remove or correct a value only when there is strong evidence of invalid data.
- Log all removals or corrections.

10.4 Data Lineage

The project must retain:
- Original raw dataset unchanged.
- Cleaned dataset.
- Feature-engineered dataset.
- Training metadata.
- Model configuration.
- Threshold configuration.
- Generated predictions.
- Model version and creation timestamp.

10.5 Ethical and Operational Guardrails

The dashboard must state:
- Alerts are decision-support signals.
- High risk does not prove a failure.
- Maintenance personnel must validate recommendations.
- Poor sensor or network data can create false alerts.
- Model results must be monitored after deployment.

11.2 Baseline Behavior Modeling

The system must learn normal behavior by:
- Machine_ID
- Operation_Mode
- Machine_ID + Operation_Mode where sufficient data exists

The baseline must use robust statistics:
- Median
- Median absolute deviation
- Interquartile range
- Rolling median
- Exponentially weighted mean
- Rolling quantiles

Fallback hierarchy:
1. Machine + mode baseline
2. Machine baseline
3. Mode baseline
4. Global baseline

A baseline must not be used when the sample count is below a configurable minimum.

11.3 Core Modeling Feature Policy

The core anomaly model should include:
- Temperature_C
- Vibration_Hz
- Power_Consumption_kW
- Network_Latency_ms
- Packet_Loss_%
- Quality_Control_Defect_Rate_%
- Production_Speed_units_per_hr
- Error_Rate_%
- Engineered deviation and trend features

The core anomaly model must exclude:
- Efficiency_Status
- Predictive_Maintenance_Score
- Raw Machine_ID as a continuous value
- Any future-derived feature
- Any risk label generated from the same model

An optional augmented experiment may include Predictive_Maintenance_Score features only if:
- It is clearly separated from the core model.
- Leakage risk is discussed.
- Results are not presented as the primary unbiased model.
- Its provenance is documented or acknowledged as unknown.

13. ANOMALY DETECTION MODEL REQUIREMENTS
===============================================================================

13.1 Required Baseline Model

Isolation Forest is the required primary baseline because it:
- Works without failure labels.
- Detects rare combinations.
- Scales to the supplied dataset.
- Produces a continuous anomaly score.

Required tunable parameters:
- n_estimators
- contamination
- max_samples
- max_features
- bootstrap
- random_state

13.2 Optional Advanced Model

An autoencoder may be developed as an optional deep-learning variant.

Requirements:
- Train primarily on a carefully defined normal subset.
- Use reconstruction error as the anomaly signal.
- Apply early stopping.
- Use a validation period later than the training period.
- Avoid overfitting.
- Report architecture, activation functions, optimizer, learning rate, batch size, and epochs.
- Compare against Isolation Forest.
- Do not use deep learning only for presentation value.

13.3 Optional Supporting Models

The project may evaluate:
- Local Outlier Factor for offline comparison
- One-Class SVM on a sampled or reduced feature space
- Robust covariance where assumptions are reasonable
- PCA reconstruction error
- Clustering-distance methods

These are supporting experiments, not mandatory production models.

13.4 Ensemble Strategy

Recommended production score:
- Isolation Forest score
- Optional autoencoder reconstruction score
- Persistence score
- Trend-escalation score

The final score may use a weighted ensemble, but:
- Every weight must be documented.
- The result must be normalized.
- The score must remain interpretable.
- Model disagreement must be shown.
- A simpler model should be preferred if performance is comparable.

13.5 Machine-Aware Training Strategy

Preferred options:
- Global model trained on machine- and mode-normalized features.
- Separate models by mode when sufficient data exists.
- Machine-specific thresholds rather than 50 completely separate models unless evidence supports separate models.

This balances personalization, sample size, maintainability, and generalization.

===============================================================================
14. TIME-AWARE TRAINING AND VALIDATION
===============================================================================

Random train-test splitting alone is prohibited for the primary evaluation.

Required approach:
- Sort data chronologically.
- Use an earlier period for training.
- Use a later period for validation.
- Use the latest period for test or backtesting.
- Fit scalers and baselines using training data only.
- Prevent future information from entering historical features.
- Recalculate rolling features using past data only.
- Document all time boundaries.

Recommended backtesting:
- Expanding-window or rolling-window evaluation.
- Compare alert performance across multiple time blocks.
- Assess threshold stability over time.

15. ANOMALY SCORE AND RISK CLASSIFICATION
===============================================================================

15.1 Score Direction

The application must use:
- 0 = least abnormal
- 100 = most abnormal

Native model scores must be transformed consistently.

15.2 Default Risk Mapping

Initial configurable percentile-based mapping:
- Low Risk: below the 90th percentile
- Medium Risk: 90th to below 97.5th percentile
- High Risk: 97.5th percentile and above

Thresholds should preferably be calibrated by machine-mode group when sample size is sufficient.

15.3 Persistence Override

A single extreme observation may be high risk, but persistent medium risk may also require escalation.

Example rules:
- Medium risk persisting across a configurable recent window can escalate to High.
- High risk with weak evidence and immediate normalization may be downgraded to “Transient High”.
- Model disagreement lowers evidence strength.
- Multiple abnormal signals plus worsening trend raises evidence strength.

15.4 Risk Terminology

Low Risk:
- Behavior is within expected range.
- Action: Continue monitoring.

Medium Risk:
- Early deviation or persistent instability detected.
- Action: Review recent history and schedule inspection if trend continues.

High Risk:
- Strong, persistent, or rapidly escalating deviation detected.
- Action: Prioritize inspection and verify machine and sensor condition.

15.5 No-Failure Disclaimer

Risk level is not a confirmed failure probability unless a separately trained, labeled failure-probability model is later introduced.

17. PROXY EARLY-WARNING VALIDATION
===============================================================================

Because confirmed failure timestamps are absent, early-warning performance must use transparent proxy events.

17.1 Permitted Proxy Events

A future deterioration event may be defined as:
- Transition to Efficiency_Status = Low
- Significant future production-speed decline
- Significant future error-rate increase
- Significant future defect-rate increase
- Entry into Maintenance mode
- Strong future decline in Predictive_Maintenance_Score, used only as an external comparison

17.2 Example Prediction Horizons

Evaluate whether elevated anomaly risk occurs before deterioration within:
- 1 hour
- 6 hours
- 24 hours
- 72 hours

The final horizons should reflect actual observation density.

17.3 Proxy Early Warning Lead Time

Definition:
Time between the first sustained elevated-risk signal and the later proxy deterioration event.

Display requirement:
The dashboard and paper must label this as “Proxy Early Warning Lead Time,” not confirmed failure lead time.

17.4 Evaluation Metrics

- Precision at top K alerts
- Recall of future deterioration events
- Alert lead time
- False-alert rate
- Alerts per machine per day
- Persistent-alert precision
- Average score before deterioration
- Score separation between stable and deteriorating periods
- Stability across time windows
- Stability across operation modes
- Model agreement
- Calibration by risk percentile

22. ALERT EXPLANATION REQUIREMENTS
===============================================================================

Each Medium or High alert must answer:

- Which machine is affected?
- When did the abnormal behavior start?
- Is it persistent or transient?
- Which signals are most abnormal?
- How far are they from the machine’s normal behavior?
- Is the risk increasing?
- Is network instability involved?
- Is production or quality being affected?
- What action is recommended?
- How strong is the evidence?

Example plain-language alert:

“Machine 17 has remained above its normal risk range for four recent observations. Vibration instability, rising error rate, and lower production speed are the main contributors. The pattern is escalating rather than a single spike. Inspect vibration sources and verify sensor calibration during the next maintenance window.”

The application must avoid unsupported wording such as:
“Machine 17 will fail in six hours.”

23. RECOMMENDATION ENGINE REQUIREMENTS
===============================================================================

Recommendations must be rule-assisted and evidence-linked.

Examples:

High vibration deviation:
- Inspect bearings, alignment, mounting, and rotating components.
- Verify vibration sensor calibration.

High temperature and power:
- Inspect cooling, lubrication, load, and electrical demand.
- Verify operating mode.

High error and defect rates:
- Inspect process setup, tooling, calibration, and material flow.
- Review recent production changes.

High network latency and packet loss:
- Verify connectivity, gateway health, interference, and sensor transmission.
- Determine whether the anomaly is operational or communication-related.

Production-speed decline with rising risk:
- Consider controlled load reduction.
- Schedule inspection before further throughput loss.

Data-quality concern:
- Validate sensor and timestamp integrity before physical intervention.

Recommendations must be presented as inspection guidance, not definitive diagnosis.

33. RISKS AND MITIGATIONS
===============================================================================

Risk: No explicit failure label.
Mitigation:
Use unsupervised anomaly detection and transparent proxy validation. Recommend collecting actual failure and repair events.

Risk: Existing Predictive_Maintenance_Score may cause leakage.
Mitigation:
Exclude it from the core model and use it only for contextual comparison or a clearly separated experiment.

Risk: Efficiency_Status is heavily imbalanced.
Mitigation:
Do not use it as the anomaly target. Use stratified contextual analysis and appropriate metrics.

Risk: Irregular per-machine sampling.
Mitigation:
Use timestamp-aware features and report observation coverage.

Risk: Duplicate machine timestamps.
Mitigation:
Audit and resolve explicitly.

Risk: Excessive false alerts.
Mitigation:
Use machine-mode thresholds, persistence logic, model agreement, and alert-rate monitoring.

Risk: Anomaly does not equal failure.
Mitigation:
Use careful wording and require human validation.

Risk: Network issues may look like machine issues.
Mitigation:
Separate network, sensor, and production contributor groups.

Risk: Dashboard becomes overcrowded.
Mitigation:
Use a simple executive layer and progressive disclosure.

Risk: Autoencoder adds complexity without value.
Mitigation:
Retain only if it improves time-aware proxy performance or alert quality.

Risk: Simulated live data is misunderstood.
Mitigation:
Display a persistent “Simulation” badge and document the source.

38. FINAL PRODUCT POSITIONING
===============================================================================

FactoryGuard 6G is an anomaly-driven predictive-maintenance decision-support platform for intelligent manufacturing. It differs from a conventional efficiency-classification project because it focuses on emerging machine-health risk, temporal escalation, early warning, maintenance prioritization, and explainable operational action.

Its strongest value is not merely detecting that a record is unusual. Its value is converting machine, production, quality, and network deviations into a clear operational workflow:

Detect
→ Validate
→ Explain
→ Prioritize
→ Inspect
→ Compare recovery
→ Improve future monitoring

The final solution must demonstrate analytical depth, technical integrity, responsible model communication, and practical value for manufacturing decision-makers.
